# Example: Sleep Tracker Offer Score

This is a complete scored example for a "Build a Sleep Tracker App" offer. Use it as a reference for tone, depth of justification, and calculation format.

---

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════
  OFFER VALIDATION REPORT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Offer:    Build & Sell a Sleep Tracker App
  Audience: Aspiring indie developers who want to build a real
            product and learn to monetise it, but have never
            shipped an app before.
  Date:     2026-02-20

───────────────────────────────────────────────────
  VALUE EQUATION SCORES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Dream Outcome:          7/10
  Building a real, working app and learning to sell it is a
  compelling outcome for aspiring developers. However, a "sleep
  tracker" is a niche topic — the dream is the skill (build and
  sell), not the specific app. If the offer leaned harder into
  "become someone who ships and sells software," it would score
  higher.

  Perceived Likelihood:   8/10
  The offer includes a clear methodology (ODD framework +
  SpecKit), a step-by-step book, and working code the buyer can
  run. The process is transparent — the buyer can see exactly
  what they will build at each stage. Strong proof of concept
  because the finished app exists and is demonstrated.

  Time Delay:             8/10
  The "build in a weekend" promise means first results are
  visible within hours of starting. The buyer will have a running
  app on their device by the end of day one. This is a genuine
  quick win — they can show someone a working screen almost
  immediately.

  Effort & Sacrifice:     6/10
  The buyer needs to install Flutter, Claude Code, and configure
  multiple development tools before writing a single line of code.
  The book guides them through it, but the setup is still a
  barrier — especially for someone who has never shipped an app.
  Once set up, the process is well-guided, but the initial
  friction is real.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────
  OVERALL VALUE SCORE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Formula: (Dream × Likelihood) / ((11 - TimeDelay) × (11 - Effort))
  Calculation: (7 × 8) / ((11 - 8) × (11 - 6))
             = 56 / (3 × 5)
             = 56 / 15
             = 3.73

  Overall Value: 3.7/10

  Moderate offer with strong methodology and fast results, but
  the setup effort drags the overall score down. The buyer
  believes it will work and can see results quickly — reducing
  friction is the clearest path to a higher score.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────
  RECOMMENDATIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────

  1. REDUCE EFFORT & SACRIFICE [Highest Priority]
     Current score: 6/10
     Target score: 8/10
     Action: Provide a pre-configured project template and a
     one-command setup script (e.g. `curl setup.sh | bash` or a
     Docker container with everything installed). Currently the
     buyer must install Flutter, Claude Code, and configure
     multiple tools manually. A single setup command that handles
     all dependencies would remove the biggest friction point.
     Also consider a browser-based IDE option (Gitpod/Codespaces)
     so the buyer can start building without installing anything
     locally.

  2. SHARPEN THE DREAM OUTCOME [Second Priority]
     Current score: 7/10
     Target score: 9/10
     Action: Reframe the offer around the identity transformation
     rather than the specific app. Instead of "Build a Sleep
     Tracker App," position it as "Ship Your First Product This
     Weekend." The sleep tracker becomes the vehicle, not the
     destination. The buyer's real dream is becoming someone who
     builds and sells software — lean into that identity shift
     in the headline, landing page, and opening chapter.

  3. ADD A VISIBLE PROGRESS SYSTEM [General]
     Action: Build a checklist or progress tracker into the book
     itself — something the buyer can tick off as they complete
     each stage. This serves two purposes: it makes the quick
     wins visible (reinforcing the Time Delay score) and it
     breaks the effort into small, manageable chunks (improving
     the Effort score). Consider a "launch readiness" percentage
     that increases as the buyer completes each chapter.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

## Why These Scores?

This example illustrates several scoring principles:

**Dream Outcome at 7, not 9**: The offer teaches a genuinely useful skill (building and selling apps), but the packaging focuses on the vehicle (sleep tracker) rather than the destination (becoming a shipped-product developer). A reframe could push this to 8–9 without changing the content.

**Perceived Likelihood at 8**: This is the offer's strongest component. A named methodology, working code, and a transparent process give the buyer real confidence. The only thing preventing a 9 is the lack of testimonials or case studies from people who have completed the process.

**Time Delay at 8**: "Build in a weekend" with a working screen on day one is genuinely fast. It doesn't score 9–10 because the *selling* part (the second half of the promise) takes longer — the buyer won't have revenue on day one.

**Effort at 6, not 4**: The process is well-guided and the book walks through each step, which lifts it above a raw "figure it out yourself" experience. But the multi-tool setup and new-concept learning curve are real barriers for the target avatar (someone who has never shipped an app).

**Overall at 3.7**: This might feel low for what seems like a solid offer. That's the denominator at work — even moderate friction (Effort = 6) creates significant drag because it gets inverted to 5 in the denominator. This is exactly why reducing effort is the highest-priority recommendation: moving Effort from 6 to 8 would change the calculation to 56 / (3 × 3) = 56 / 9 = 6.2 — nearly doubling the score.
